1. How This HOA Works

▼

Development Period Control

Parten Ranch is currently in the "Development Period" where the builder, HM Parten Development, Inc., retains significant control over the community. This has important implications for your rights.

Key Point About Declarant Control

During the development period, the Parten Ranch Reviewer (appointed by the developer) acts "solely in Declarant's interest" (Design Guidelines Section I.B). This is different from normal HOA governance where the board acts in the interest of all residents.

Governing Documents Hierarchy

There are three key documents that govern Parten Ranch. They form a hierarchy:

  1. Development Area Declaration (recorded in Hays County) - the master document
  2. Design Guidelines (referenced in the Declaration) - detailed requirements
  3. Community Manual - day-to-day operational rules

If these documents conflict, the Declaration controls, then the Design Guidelines, then the Manual.

Three Layers of Regulation

Your property is governed by three layers of rules:

Layer 1: Federal & State Law - Texas Property Code Chapters 202, 209, and other state laws. These CANNOT be overridden by HOA documents.
Layer 2: Local Government - City of Dripping Springs and Hays County ordinances, building codes, and zoning. These often take precedence over HOA rules.
Layer 3: HOA Documents - The Declaration, Design Guidelines, and Community Manual. These must comply with state and local laws.

Important: Architectural Review Protections

Texas Property Code §209.00505 provides important protections for architectural review requests. However, during the declarant control period, these protections may be limited. Once the development period ends and the declarant relinquishes control, these protections become your right:

  • Written notice of denial within 30 days
  • Specific citation of the governing document provision
  • Right to appeal to the board
  • Reasonable time to cure violations before fines

The Development Period and Your Options

The longer the development period lasts, the more restrictive your architectural review process can be. Once declarant control ends:

  • The board must include resident members
  • Standard §209.00505 protections apply fully
  • Approval/denial decisions become more subject to legal scrutiny

Is This Actually a Rule?

"The HOA has complete discretion to deny any request for any reason." - This is NOT accurate. Even during the development period, the Parten Ranch Reviewer must cite specific provisions from the governing documents. If a rule doesn't appear in the Design Guidelines or Declaration, it cannot be used to deny a request.

2. Pools, Spas & Hot Tubs

▼

Pool Type Requirements (Design Guidelines Section H)

If you plan to install a pool, it must meet these documented requirements:

In-Ground Only: All pools must be in-ground or "balanced cut and fill" (fill material used on-site). Above-ground pools are not permitted.
City Permits First: You must obtain City of Dripping Springs construction permits BEFORE submitting to the HOA for architectural review. The HOA will not review applications without proof of city permits.

Setback Requirements

  • Pool: no closer than 5 feet from any property line
  • Pool deck: no closer than 5 feet from property line

Pool Features & Equipment

Infinity Edge Pools: No more than 3 feet of exposed concrete visible (must be integral to landscape design)
Water Features: Rock waterfalls and slides must not exceed 6 feet in height
Equipment Screening: All pool equipment (pumps, heaters, filters) must be screened and not visible from the street or from adjacent lots
Drainage: Pool drains must connect to street drainage systems. No draining onto neighboring property.
Lot Placement: Pools must be in the rear portion of the lot

Fencing & Water Fill Requirements

  • Fencing is required and must meet all government regulations (see Fences & Walls section)
  • No water is permitted in the pool until proper fencing is installed and approved by the city

Texas Health & Safety Code Requirements (Chapter 757)

Texas law mandates specific pool safety requirements that the HOA cannot prohibit:

Required Pool Barriers

  • Barrier (fence/wall) must be at least 48 inches tall
  • No gaps allowing a 4-inch sphere to pass through
  • Barrier must be at least 20 inches from pool edge
  • Gates must be self-closing and self-latching
  • Latch release must be at least 54 inches above ground
  • Chain-link fencing is PROHIBITED for pools built after January 1, 1994

Texas Property Code Protection

§202.022: Pool Enclosure Protection

The HOA CANNOT prohibit pool enclosures that comply with state safety codes. This means you have the right to install safety features required by Texas law, and the HOA cannot deny them or impose additional restrictions that conflict with safety requirements.

City of Dripping Springs Fence Permits

The City of Dripping Springs requires a fence permit for pool enclosures. Your fence design must meet state requirements, and the HOA cannot impose stricter safety requirements than state law allows.

Is This Actually a Rule?

"Your pool fence must be solid wood with no gaps." - NOT CLEARLY DOCUMENTED for pools. The Design Guidelines Section G covers general fencing and requires wrought iron/decorative metal, but §202.022 protects your right to use safety-compliant materials. Check with the City of Dripping Springs on what materials they approve for pool safety enclosures.

Application Checklist for Pools

  1. Contact City of Dripping Springs Building Department for permit requirements
  2. Obtain all city permits before submitting to HOA
  3. Submit HOA architectural review request with city permits and detailed plans
  4. Include setback measurements and equipment screening details
  5. Allow 30 days for HOA review
  6. Obtain city approval before filling pool

3. Pergolas, Patios & Outdoor Structures

▼

Accessory Structure Limits (Design Guidelines Section F)

The Design Guidelines specifically define accessory structures (sheds, storage buildings) with this size limit:

Base Size Limit: 10' x 12' (120 square feet) - UNLESS the Parten Ranch Reviewer approves a larger structure in advance and in writing.

This is a critical point: the design guidelines explicitly state "unless otherwise approved in advance and in writing by the Parten Ranch Reviewer." This means larger structures CAN be approved.

Material & Design Requirements

  • Roof materials must match the main house
  • Must have pitched roof matching the main house roof pitch
  • Siding and paint colors must match the main house

Setback Requirements

  • Minimum 5 feet from interior lot lines
  • No accessory structures on lots adjacent to greenbelt or open space areas

Quantity Limit

Generally, only ONE accessory structure per lot is permitted. However, the "otherwise approved" language means additional structures may be possible with written approval.

What About Pergolas? The Gray Area

Is This Actually a Rule?

"Pergolas have a specific size limit" - NOT FOUND IN ANY GOVERNING DOCUMENT. The only documented size limit (10'x12') applies to "accessory structures" such as sheds (Design Guidelines Section C.7). Pergolas are never specifically defined or mentioned in the Design Guidelines. The "otherwise approved in advance and in writing" clause suggests larger structures may be approvable on a case-by-case basis. If a size restriction is cited for a pergola, request the specific governing document section number.

Is This Actually a Rule?

"Pergolas must have a pitched or angled roof" - PARTIALLY DOCUMENTED BUT NOT SPECIFIC TO PERGOLAS. The Design Guidelines Section D.2 states that "the main roof pitch of the primary residence" must be minimum 6:12, and notes that "dormers, porches and other similar accessory structures attached to the primary residence shall be exempt from this requirement." Since pergolas are not specifically defined in the Design Guidelines and freestanding pergolas are not attached to the primary residence, this roof pitch requirement may not apply. If this restriction is cited, ask for the specific provision that extends roof pitch requirements to pergolas.

Patios (Uncovered Surfaces)

The Design Guidelines do not specify restrictions for patio areas (pavers, concrete without roofing). These are generally not considered "accessory structures." However, always submit a request if you plan to extend more than 500 square feet or if it's visible from the street.

Attached vs. Detached Structures

The guidelines treat attached structures (extending from the house) differently from detached structures. An attached pergola may have different requirements than a detached one. When submitting, clearly specify whether your structure is attached or detached.

Application Strategy

  1. Request the written definition of "pergola" from the Parten Ranch Reviewer
  2. If denied, request the specific Design Guidelines section number supporting the denial
  3. If citing a size limit, request documentation of that limit
  4. Remember: "otherwise approved in advance and in writing" means you can request exception approval
  5. Document everything in writing via email

4. Fences & Walls

▼

Fencing Material Requirements (Design Guidelines Section G)

Required Materials: All fencing must be wrought iron or decorative metal only. The metal must be black coated.
Prohibited: Wood fencing is prohibited in Parten Ranch.

Fencing Height

  • Standard height: 6 feet
  • This applies to side and rear yard fences

Fencing Placement Requirements

Corner Lots: Side yard fences must not be closer than 8 feet to the street right-of-way
Rear Placement: Side fences placed no further to the rear than the midpoint of the primary building
Front Yards: Fencing of front yards is prohibited

Greenbelt & Open Space Restrictions

For lots adjacent to greenbelt or open space areas:

  • NO GATES are permitted in fences adjacent to greenbelts
  • This prevents private access to common areas

Solid Walls Prohibition

Solid walls enclosing an entire lot/site are prohibited. However, partial walls or walls integrated with landscape design may be approvable.

City of Dripping Springs Fence Permits

All fencing requires a permit from the City of Dripping Springs. The city may have additional requirements beyond the HOA rules. Check with the city first for:

  • Setback requirements
  • Height limitations
  • Material approval
  • Gate regulations

Application Process for Fences

  1. Verify city requirements with Dripping Springs Building Department
  2. Prepare detailed fence plans with measurements and materials
  3. Submit to Parten Ranch Reviewer with city requirements documentation
  4. Specify material color (black coated metal)
  5. Include lot survey showing setbacks
  6. Allow 30 days for approval
  7. Obtain city permit before installation

Security Fencing (2nd Supplement Community Manual Tab C)

Security Fence Style: Security fencing must be ornamental wrought iron, black in color
Security Fence Specs: Standard security fence dimensions: 5 feet tall picket style with a 1-inch picket width, 4-inch spacing between pickets, and maximum 6-foot post-to-post span

Pool Fencing (2nd Supplement Community Manual Tab C)

Pool Fence Material: Pool fencing must be transparent mesh safety fencing, black in color
Pool Fence Height: Maximum 6 feet tall. Must also meet Texas Health & Safety Code requirements (minimum 48 inches, no 4-inch sphere gaps)
Not Climbable: Pool fencing must be designed so it is not climbable by children

Slats & Privacy Inserts

Slats Prohibited: Slats or privacy inserts in wrought iron fencing are NOT permitted. The open design of wrought iron/decorative metal fencing must be maintained.

Common Fence Scenarios

Pool Fencing: Must meet both HOA standards (wrought iron/metal, 6 feet) AND Texas Health & Safety Code (48 inches minimum, no 4-inch sphere gaps). See Pool section for details.
Dog Containment: Fencing must use approved wrought iron/metal. The structure must meet state safety requirements (48 inches minimum) and city permit requirements.
Decorative Walls (partial): Low walls (under 3 feet) may be acceptable if designed as part of landscape architecture and use approved materials. Submit with landscape plan.

5. Landscaping & Yard

▼

Initial Landscape Requirements (Design Guidelines Section VII)

When you build or significantly update landscaping, these minimum requirements apply:

Sod Coverage: Front and side yards must be fully sodded
Trees: Minimum 2 hardwood trees (4 trees on corner lots)
Shrubs: Minimum 10 five-gallon shrubs

Grass Recommendations & Maintenance

The Design Guidelines recommend specific grasses for the Parten Ranch climate:

  • Buffalo grass - recommended for drought tolerance
  • Zoysia - recommended
  • Bermuda grass - recommended and commonly used
  • St. Augustine grass - PROHIBITED in Parten Ranch

Maintenance Standard: Bermuda and Buffalo grass should be maintained at 2-2.5 inches height

Mulch Requirements

  • Minimum 2 inches of mulch in all shrub and planting bed areas
  • Maintains moisture and reduces water needs

Native & Drought-Tolerant Plants

The Design Guidelines emphasize native and drought-tolerant plants suitable for Central Texas. This is encouraged by both the HOA and Texas law.

Texas Property Code §202.007: Xeriscaping Protection

The HOA CANNOT prohibit or restrict:

  • Xeriscaping (drought-resistant landscaping)
  • Native plants
  • Water-saving irrigation systems
  • Rain barrels for water collection
  • Composting systems on your property

The HOA also cannot fine for natural browning during water restrictions.

Lawn Drought Protection (HB 517 - Effective 2025)

Texas Lawn Dormancy Protection

The HOA CANNOT fine you for brown or dormant grass during periods of:

  • City or county-issued watering restrictions
  • Voluntary water conservation periods
  • Up to 60 days AFTER restrictions or conservation periods end

This protects you when your lawn goes dormant during drought conditions.

Plant & Decoration Restrictions

Artificial Plants/Plastic Decorations: Prohibited in front yard (except holiday decorations during appropriate seasons)
Birdbaths: Allowed in front or side yards without approval
Sculptures/Fountains: Require written approval before installation
Garden Swings/Benches: Allowed on front porches; front lawns or driveways require approval

Landscaping Change Policy

If you plan significant changes to an already-approved landscape plan:

  • Submit a new landscape plan for approval
  • The HOA may deny major changes that deviate from the original approved design
  • Minor maintenance and seasonal changes don't require reapproval

Application for Landscaping Changes

  1. If minor changes (different shrubs, add trees): Submit written request
  2. If major changes (full replant, different layout): Submit new landscape plan
  3. Include plant varieties, placement, and hardscape elements
  4. Verify your plan uses approved grass types and native/drought-tolerant plants
  5. Include any fountains, sculptures, or structures for approval

Common Landscaping Scenarios

Removing Lawn for Decorative Rock: Not explicitly prohibited, but must maintain overall lot appearance. Consider xeriscaping with drought-tolerant plants as alternative.
Adding a Rain Barrel: This is protected by §202.007. The HOA cannot prohibit it. However, screen it from street view if possible.
Vegetable Garden: Not specifically addressed in guidelines. Small gardens are generally acceptable; large operations may require approval.

6. Solar Panels & Energy

▼

Texas Law Protection for Solar Panels

Texas Property Code §202.010: Solar Panel Protection

The HOA CANNOT prohibit or unduly restrict solar panel installation on your property. Specifically:

  • HOA cannot deny solar panel installation without a reasonable basis documented in writing
  • HOA cannot prohibit solar panels that don't extend above the roofline
  • Any restrictions that prevent installation or significantly increase cost are VOID
  • HOA cannot require architectural review or approval that is unreasonably withheld

What This Means for Parten Ranch Residents

Even during the declarant control period, the HOA cannot:

  • Completely ban solar panels
  • Require impossible design standards for panels
  • Charge excessive fees for review
  • Require panels be hidden from view if roofline-mounted

Reasonable Design Standards

The HOA can request reasonable architectural design considerations:

  • Panels should be integrated into roof design if possible
  • Visible panels on south or west-facing roofs are generally acceptable
  • Ground-mounted systems may face more scrutiny (setback requirements, visibility)

Energy-Efficient Roofing Materials

Texas Property Code §202.011: Energy-Efficient Shingles

The HOA CANNOT prohibit or restrict:

  • Wind-resistant roofing materials
  • Hail-resistant roofing materials
  • Energy-efficient shingles or roofing systems

This protects your right to upgrade to modern, energy-efficient roofing that saves money and protects against weather.

Design Guidelines Roof Requirements

The Design Guidelines Section D specifies roofing requirements, but these cannot override state law protections:

  • Minimum 30-year asphalt composition shingles with "Weatheredwood" color default
  • This doesn't prohibit upgraded materials with similar appearance
  • Energy-efficient shingles in similar color tones should be approvable

Is This Actually a Rule?

"We prohibit solar panels in Parten Ranch because they're not aesthetically appropriate." - This is likely not enforceable under §202.010. Request the specific written provision from the Design Guidelines or Declaration. If none exists, the prohibition is likely illegal.

Application Process for Solar Panels

  1. Prepare design drawings showing panel placement and appearance from street view
  2. If roofline-mounted and not visible, note this in your request
  3. Include solar contractor information and system specs
  4. Submit to Parten Ranch Reviewer with note referencing §202.010
  5. If denied, request written explanation citing specific Design Guidelines provision
  6. If denial seems unreasonable, consult the Texas State Law Library HOA guide or an attorney

Application Process for Energy-Efficient Roofing

  1. When replacing your roof, specify energy-efficient materials
  2. Include color samples showing similarity to "Weatheredwood" standard
  3. If denied, request written provision citing specific restriction
  4. Note that §202.011 protects your right to use wind/hail-resistant materials

7. Roofs & Exterior

▼

Masonry & Exterior Material Requirements (Design Guidelines Section D)

Masonry Requirement: 85% masonry minimum on all visible exterior surfaces
Street-Facing Requirement: 100% masonry on all street-facing elevations (no stucco or other materials on front facade)
Stucco Limitation: No more than 50% stucco without specific approval from the Parten Ranch Reviewer

Masonry Colors

  • No gray masonry tones
  • Warm earth tones required
  • Aligns with Hill Country aesthetic

Roof Specifications (Design Guidelines Section D)

Roof Pitch: Minimum 6:12 (6 inches vertical for every 12 inches horizontal) for primary residence

Exception: Dormers, porches, and other similar accessory structures attached to the primary residence are exempt from the 6:12 pitch requirement

Roofing Material: Minimum 30-year asphalt composition shingles
Color: "Weatheredwood" is the default color standard

Prohibited Roof Types

The Design Guidelines specifically prohibit these roof styles:

  • Mansard roofs
  • Gambrel roofs
  • Shed roofs (on primary residences)
  • Flat roofs

Foundation Requirements

Foundation Exposure: Maximum 24 inches of foundation wall visible above grade (ground level)

Color Restrictions

  • No vivid or bright colors
  • Earth tones and muted colors only
  • Consistent with Hill Country community aesthetic

Energy-Efficient Upgrades (Protected by State Law)

Energy-Efficient Shingles (§202.011)

When replacing your roof, you can use energy-efficient materials. The HOA cannot prohibit:

  • Wind-resistant shingles
  • Hail-resistant shingles
  • Cool roof technology (reflective coatings)

These must be in an approved color, but the HOA cannot ban them for being energy-efficient.

Application Process for Exterior Changes

  1. For roof replacement: Submit roofing material samples in "Weatheredwood" color range or similar
  2. For stucco upgrades: Submit plans showing placement and color samples
  3. For large masonry changes: Submit elevation renderings
  4. For foundation work: Include before/after plans
  5. Reference relevant Design Guidelines section in your request

Common Exterior Scenarios

Replacing Roof with Energy-Efficient Shingles: Approved if color is similar to Weatheredwood. §202.011 protects your right to use these materials.
Upgrading Foundation Exposure (Adding Height): Allowed up to 24 inches. Going beyond this requires approval. Often involves regrading your lot, which requires city approval.
Adding Dormers: Dormers are exempt from the 6:12 pitch requirement. Design should be compatible with primary roof pitch.

8. Garages & Driveways

▼

Garage Requirements (Design Guidelines Section I)

Minimum Coverage: Enclosed garage required for minimum 2 cars
Carports Prohibited: No open or covered carports; all vehicle storage must be fully enclosed

Garage Door Specifications

  • Garage doors must be electric-operated (manual doors not permitted)
  • Material: Metal or wood doors acceptable
  • Design should be compatible with house architecture

Driveway Requirements (Design Guidelines Section J)

Surface Material: Concrete required
Prohibited Materials: Aggregate driveways are not permitted
Staining: No stained concrete on driveways or sidewalks

Driveway Design

While the Design Guidelines don't extensively regulate driveway design, they should:

  • Be compatible with overall lot grading and drainage
  • Provide adequate drainage (no pooling)
  • Be maintained in good condition

No Open Automobile Storage

Prohibition: No open automobile storage is permitted. All vehicles must be garaged or on the driveway. No vehicles parked on the lawn.

Application Process

  1. For garage door replacement: Submit door sample and color information
  2. For new driveway: Submit concrete specifications and design
  3. For driveway expansion/extension: Include survey showing placement
  4. Verify with City of Dripping Springs for any driveway access requirements

Common Garage Scenarios

RV/Boat Parking: Cannot be stored on your lot unless garaged. The prohibition on "open automobile storage" extends to RVs and boats.
Garage Conversion: You cannot convert an enclosed garage to living space (this would eliminate the required 2-car garage).
Garage Door Openers: Electric openers are required. Manual garage doors are not permitted.

9. Basketball Goals, Playscapes & Sports Equipment

▼

Basketball Goals (Design Guidelines Sections I & J)

Approval Required: Written approval required before any basketball goal installation
Permanent Goals Only: Metal pole, permanently mounted goals are permitted
Portable Goals Prohibited: Portable/temporary basketball goals are NOT permitted

Basketball Goal Placement Requirements

  • Must be on the side of the driveway (not in center)
  • Minimum 25 feet back from curb/street
  • Must be in rear yard area, not visible from front

Trampolines (Design Guidelines Section J)

Approval Required: Written approval required before installation

Trampoline Requirements

  • Minimum 5 feet from any property line
  • No direct or indirect lighting on trampoline
  • Must be in rear yard, not visible from front

Sport Courts & Tennis Courts

Sport Courts Prohibited: Sport courts (including basketball courts, tennis courts, and similar structures) are not permitted

Playscapes & Play Equipment (Design Guidelines Section J)

Approval Required: Written approval required before installation

Playscape Design Requirements

  • Must use earth tone colors (no bright primary colors like red, blue, yellow)
  • Must be screened from public view (not visible from street)
  • Must be in rear yard area

Application Process for Sports Equipment

  1. Submit written request for approval
  2. Include detailed placement plan with measurements from property lines and street
  3. For basketball goals: Include pole specifications and mounting details
  4. For trampolines: Include size and safety specifications
  5. For playscapes: Include color renderings showing earth tone colors
  6. Allow 30 days for approval

Common Sports Equipment Scenarios

Portable Basketball Hoops: NOT PERMITTED. Must be permanently mounted. If you have a portable hoop, the HOA can require its removal.
Swing Sets: Generally treated as playscapes. Earth tone colors required. Must be screened from street view. Typically acceptable in rear yards.
Sandbox: Allowed as part of playscape if screened from view and using earth tone containment.
Sports Lighting: Lights directing onto sports equipment (basketball hoop lights) are prohibited. Avoid floodlighting that shines on courts or play areas.

10. Flags, Signs & Displays

▼

Flag Protection Under Texas Law

Texas Property Code §202.012: Flag Display Protection

The HOA CANNOT prohibit or unreasonably restrict display of:

  • United States flag
  • Texas state flag
  • United States Armed Forces flags

The HOA MAY impose reasonable restrictions on size, placement, and manner of display, but cannot ban these flags altogether.

Religious Display Protection

Texas Property Code §202.018: Religious Display Protection

The HOA CANNOT prohibit religious displays on your property. This includes:

  • Religious symbols
  • Holiday religious decorations (nativity scenes, menorahs, etc.)
  • Religious yard signs or expressions

The HOA cannot regulate these based on religious content.

Lemonade Stand Protection

Texas Property Code §202.020: Children's Lemonade Stands

The HOA CANNOT prohibit children from operating lemonade stands or similar temporary food/beverage sales. This is a protected temporary use on your property.

Real Estate For Sale/Lease Signs (Development Area Declaration Section 2.10)

For Sale Signs: Permitted and must comply with the Development Area Declaration Section 2.10
For Rent Signs: Prohibited in Parten Ranch

Other Signs & Displays

Approval Required: All signs other than flags, religious displays, for-sale signs, and lemonade stands require written approval from the Parten Ranch Reviewer

Examples of Signs Requiring Approval

  • Business signs on property
  • Political signs (though constitutionality of banning these is questionable)
  • Custom decorative signs
  • Address signs (if ornate or large)

Application Process for Signs

  1. If it's a protected flag or religious display: No approval needed, but maintain reasonable appearance
  2. If it's a for-sale sign: Comply with Development Area Declaration requirements
  3. If it's another type of sign: Submit request with design specifications and placement information
  4. Document approval in writing via email

Common Sign Scenarios

American Flag Display: Protected. You can display the US flag without HOA approval. The HOA can request reasonable flagpole design standards.
Holiday Decorations: Temporary holiday decorations are typically allowed. Permanent holiday displays (year-round nativity scene) may require approval.
Business Signs (Home Business): Requires approval. The HOA can impose restrictions to maintain residential character.
Address Numbers: Decorative address signs may require approval if large or unusual. Standard address markers are typically acceptable.

11. Security & Generators

▼

Security Cameras & Motion Sensors Protection

Texas Property Code §202.023: Security Device Protection

The HOA CANNOT prohibit or unreasonably restrict:

  • Security cameras on your property
  • Motion sensors and detection devices
  • Perimeter fencing for security purposes

The HOA MAY impose reasonable restrictions on appearance and placement, but cannot ban security devices altogether.

Security Camera Installation

You can install security cameras on your property. Reasonable restrictions might include:

  • Cameras should not point directly onto neighboring properties (privacy concerns)
  • Camera placement should be minimally visible from street if possible
  • Associated wiring should be concealed when feasible

Standby Electric Generators Protection

Texas Property Code §202.019: Standby Generator Protection

The HOA CANNOT prohibit standby electric generators on your property. The HOA MAY:

  • Regulate the placement and screening of generators
  • Require generators be placed in rear yard or screened from view
  • Regulate noise levels

However, the HOA cannot completely ban generators or impose unreasonable restrictions that prevent their installation.

Generator Screening & Placement

Reasonable requirements for generators might include:

  • Placement in rear yard when possible
  • Screening with vegetation or fencing (similar to pool equipment requirements)
  • Distance from property lines (setback requirements)
  • Maintenance of landscaping around generator to reduce visibility

Application Process for Security Devices

  1. For security cameras: Install cameras. If HOA objects, request the specific Design Guidelines provision supporting their objection. Most camera installations are §202.023 protected.
  2. For generators: Submit request showing placement plan. Propose screening if rear yard placement isn't possible. Generator installation is §202.019 protected.
  3. If denied, cite the applicable Texas Property Code section

Firearm Storage

Texas Property Code §202.021: Firearm Storage

The HOA CANNOT prohibit or regulate the storage of firearms or ammunition in your home. This is protected private property use. The HOA has no authority over this.

Safe Room/Panic Room

If you plan to install a safe room or panic room in your home:

  • Interior modifications generally don't require HOA approval
  • If modifications affect exterior (additional doors, windows), submit plans for approval
  • Must comply with building codes and city permits

12. Vehicles, Parking & Storage

▼

Residential Use Only (Development Area Declaration Section 2.7)

Primary Purpose: Each property in Parten Ranch is restricted to residential use only
Vehicle Restriction: Only residential-use vehicles permitted; no commercial vehicles visible

Visible Commercial Vehicle Restriction

What this means:

  • A contractor's truck with logos/signage parked in your driveway during work is temporarily acceptable
  • A permanent business vehicle stored on your lot (delivery van, work truck) is not permitted
  • Home-based businesses cannot have client vehicles regularly parked on the property

Storage Container & Trash Management (Development Area Declaration Section 2.8)

Trash Container Storage: Trash containers must be stored out of view except on collection day
Outside Storage Restriction: No outside storage visible from the street

Temporary Storage Pods

If you need a temporary storage container (e.g., during renovation):

  • Maximum 7 days is the typical allowance
  • Must be on driveway, not street
  • Written approval required before delivery

RV, Boat & Specialty Vehicle Parking

No Open Storage: RVs, boats, trailers, and specialty vehicles cannot be parked on your lot in open view. Must be garaged or stored off-site.

This includes:

  • Motor homes
  • Travel trailers
  • Fishing boats
  • Jet skis
  • Motorcycles (in open, not garaged)
  • Utility trailers

Driveway Parking Standards

While the Design Guidelines don't extensively regulate driveway parking, the principle is that:

  • Vehicles belong in the garage or on driveway
  • Not on the lawn or side yard
  • Not more than normal household vehicles at any time

Guest Parking

Temporary guest parking is acceptable on your driveway or street in front of your home. Permanent parking of relatives' or friends' vehicles is not permitted.

Home Occupation & Vehicle Restrictions

If you operate a home business:

  • No client/customer vehicles regularly parking on your property
  • No business signage indicating home business from street
  • No commercial vehicle storage

Application Process

  1. For temporary storage pod: Submit request 2 weeks in advance with dates and purpose
  2. For permanent structure to house vehicle: Treat as accessory structure (see that section)
  3. For temporary contractor vehicles: Document the scope and timeline of work

13. Pets & Animals

▼

Household Pets (Development Area Declaration Section 2.8)

Policy: Household pets only are permitted. No farming, breeding operations, or commercial animal uses.

Local Compliance Required

All pets must comply with City of Dripping Springs and Hays County animal ordinances, which include:

  • Dogs must be registered/licensed with the city
  • Leash laws: dogs must be controlled at all times
  • Noise ordinances: dogs cannot create excessive noise
  • Maximum number of pets allowed by city ordinance

Pet Confinement

To comply with leash laws and HOA standards:

  • Dogs should not roam free on the property
  • Pet fencing (wrought iron/metal) can be installed to confine pets (see Fences section)
  • Pets should not be visible from street in situations creating nuisance

Noise & Nuisance Standard

15. Your Rights When the HOA Takes Action

▼

The Fine Process (Texas Property Code Chapter 209)

If the HOA believes you've violated the rules, here's the process they must follow:

Step 1: Violation Notice

The HOA must send you a written violation notice by certified mail. This notice MUST include:

  • Description of the alleged violation
  • Specific cite to the governing document provision you allegedly violated
  • The amount of the proposed fine
  • Notice of your right to request a hearing

Is This Actually a Rule?

"We're fining you for violating the HOA rules." Without citing the specific rule, this notice is defective. Demand they cite the exact Design Guidelines section or Declaration provision.

Step 2: Your Right to Cure

First Violation - Reasonable Time to Cure

For a first violation of any rule, the HOA must give you reasonable time to correct the violation BEFORE imposing a fine. "Reasonable time" is typically 30 days, but depends on the nature of the violation.

Examples:

  • Basketball hoop violation: 30 days to remove
  • Overgrown landscaping: 30-60 days to remedy
  • Paint color change: 60+ days depending on weather

Step 3: Right to a Hearing

Hearing Rights (§209.00505)

You have the right to request a hearing before a disinterested committee. The process:

  • Request must be made within 30 days of receiving the violation notice
  • Hearing must be held before the fine is imposed
  • Hearing must be before committee members who are disinterested (not on the board, not related to complainant)
  • You can present your side and evidence
  • You can challenge whether the violation actually exists
  • You can challenge whether the rule is in the governing documents

Step 4: Fine Amount (Fine & Enforcement Policy)

The violation notice must state the fine amount. The actual fine schedule is:

  • First violation (curable): $25 (can be avoided if you fix the violation in time)
  • Repeat violations: $50 (1st repeat), $75 (2nd), $100 (3rd), $125 (4th)
  • Continuous violations: Amount set by Board (can be daily, monthly, or quarterly)

Step 5: After a Fine

If a fine is imposed and you don't pay:

  • The HOA can place a lien on your property
  • They can pursue collection actions
  • In extreme cases, they can foreclose (sell your home)

Foreclosure Limitation (§209.006)

The HOA CANNOT foreclose on your home based solely on unpaid fines. However, they can foreclose if you owe more than a threshold (approximately $4,500) in unpaid assessments, which can include fines in some cases. Always try to resolve disputes through the hearing process.

Assessment Payment Plans (§209.008)

Right to Payment Plan

If you owe assessments or fines, the HOA MUST offer you a payment plan. The plan must:

  • Be in writing
  • Be offered even without your request (HOA must offer)
  • Last minimum 3 months
  • Allow you to pay the debt over time without losing your home

Record Access Rights (§209.0055)

Your Right to See HOA Documents

The HOA must provide you access to:

  • The Declaration and Design Guidelines
  • Board meeting minutes
  • Financial records
  • Your violation and fine history

Process: Make a written request. HOA responds within 10 business days. You get access within 15 more business days.

Board Meeting Attendance (§209.0053)

Open Meeting Rights

All HOA board meetings must be:

  • Open to all members (no closed-door sessions)
  • 72-hour written notice required before meetings
  • You can attend and speak (subject to reasonable time limits)

Challenging an Unjust Fine

If you believe a fine is unjust, here's your action plan:

  1. Document Everything: Take photos/videos of your property showing you're in compliance
  2. Request Hearing: Within 30 days of violation notice, request a hearing
  3. Prepare Your Case: Gather evidence showing either:
    • You're not violating the rule, OR
    • The rule doesn't exist in the governing documents, OR
    • The rule conflicts with state law
  4. Present Evidence at Hearing: Show photos, cite Design Guidelines sections, reference state law protections
  5. Appeal Decision: If hearing committee sides with HOA, you can appeal to the full board or pursue legal action

Attorney Fees & Legal Remedies

If the HOA sues to enforce a fine or you sue the HOA:

  • If the HOA prevails and has a clause in the Declaration, they may recover attorney fees
  • If you prevail, you may recover attorney fees and court costs
  • Consider consultation with a real estate attorney for disputes over significant amounts

Things the HOA CANNOT Prohibit (Texas Property Code Chapter 202)

These protections exist in state law and apply NOW, even during the Development Period. The HOA cannot override them:

Protected by Texas Law

  • Solar Panels (§202.010): HOA cannot prohibit solar panels. May require prior approval but cannot deny without reason. Panels extending no higher than roofline are protected.
  • Drought-Resistant Landscaping (§202.007): HOA cannot prohibit xeriscaping, native plants, rain barrels, rainwater harvesting, or composting systems.
  • Flags (§202.012): HOA cannot prohibit display of US flag, Texas flag, or US Armed Forces flags. May impose reasonable size/placement restrictions.
  • Standby Generators (§202.019): HOA cannot prohibit installation. May require reasonable restrictions on placement and screening.
  • Security Cameras & Motion Sensors (§202.023): HOA cannot prohibit security cameras, motion sensors, or perimeter fencing for security. May impose reasonable restrictions on appearance.
  • Pool Enclosures (§202.022): HOA cannot prohibit swimming pool enclosures that meet safety code requirements.
  • Religious Displays (§202.018): HOA cannot prohibit religious items on your property (entries, doors, doorframes).
  • Wind/Hail-Resistant Shingles (§202.011): HOA cannot prohibit energy-efficient or weather-resistant shingles if they are substantially similar in appearance.
  • Children's Lemonade Stands (§202.020): HOA cannot prohibit or regulate children's lemonade stands selling non-alcoholic beverages.
  • Firearms Storage (§202.021): HOA cannot regulate possession, storage, or transportation of firearms/ammunition on your own property.

2025 Legislative Updates

New Laws Effective September 1, 2025

  • Brown Lawn Protection (HB 517): HOA cannot fine you for a brown or dormant lawn during water restrictions, and this protection extends for 60 days AFTER restrictions end. Your lawn must have been in compliance before the restriction began.
  • Architectural Committee Transparency (SB 711): HOAs with 40+ lots must publicly solicit candidates for the architectural committee at least 10 days before appointments.
  • Political Gatherings (HB 621): Allows political gatherings in common areas under certain conditions.

Important Texas Law Protections to Assert

When challenging a fine or denial, you can assert these defenses:

  • "This rule is not in the Design Guidelines or Declaration" (§209.00505)
  • "This violates Texas Property Code §202" (solar panels, flags, generators, etc.)
  • "The HOA failed to give me adequate notice" (§209.00505)
  • "This rule is unreasonable under state law" (§209.006)
  • "I was not given reasonable time to cure the first violation" (§209.007)
  • "My lawn was brown due to water restrictions" (HB 517, effective Sept 2025)

18. Exterior Materials & Colors

▼

Masonry Requirements (Design Guidelines Section D)

Street-Facing Requirement: 100% masonry construction required on all street-facing elevations (front facade and sides visible from street)
Overall Requirement: At least 85% masonry minimum on all other visible exterior surfaces
Masonry Types: Only brick, stucco, and natural stone qualify as masonry for this requirement

Masonry Color Standards

  • No gray masonry tones permitted
  • Warm earth tones required
  • Colors must align with Hill Country aesthetic

Stucco Limitations

Stucco Cap: No more than 50% of the exterior may be stucco unless specifically approved by the Parten Ranch Reviewer
Pre-Approval Required: The Parten Ranch Reviewer must approve in advance both the composition and method of application for all stucco

Siding Material Requirements (Design Guidelines Section D)

Approved Siding: All siding material must be vertical lap siding manufactured from fiber-cement (Hardie-Plank brand or equivalent)
"Weatheredwood" Color: Hardie-Plank siding must be in "Weatheredwood" color unless the Parten Ranch Reviewer approves otherwise
Recessed Areas Only: Siding may only be used in recessed areas if approved in advance by the Parten Ranch Reviewer. Siding cannot be used on street-facing primary elevations.

Brick Color & Masonry Stone Repetition (Design Guidelines Section C)

The Parten Ranch Reviewer controls brick and stone color selection to maintain visual harmony across the community:

  • Similar Colors Every Third Lot: The same brick color or masonry stone can be repeated on every third lot (Pattern A, B, C, then back to A)
  • Across the Street Restriction: The same brick color or masonry stone CANNOT be placed on a lot directly across the street or diagonal from the same color
  • Closeup Proximity: Substantially similar brick or masonry colors in close proximity to each other may be denied at the Parten Ranch Reviewer's discretion

Prohibited Exterior Materials

These materials are strictly prohibited:

  • Wood shake siding (wood siding accents may be permitted if approved)
  • Gray brick or gray masonry of any type
  • Highly reflective finishes on exterior surfaces (other than non-mirrored glass)
  • Mirrored glass
  • Non-masonry materials on street-facing elevations

Paint & Color Changes

Paint Color Changes Require Approval: If you plan to repaint your home exterior in a different color, you must submit new color samples to the Parten Ranch Reviewer for approval before painting. Exterior colors must be muted earth tones consistent with Hill Country community aesthetic.

Projections & Accessories

All exterior projections must match the color of the surface from which they project, unless otherwise approved. This includes chimney flues, vents, gutters, downspouts, porches, railings, and exterior stairways.

Exception: Roofs, eaves, soffits, windows, gables, doors, garage doors, and trim work are not required to be masonry.

Application Process for Exterior Material Changes

  1. Collect material samples (brick, stone, stucco, or paint colors) in proposed colors
  2. Include elevation drawings or renderings showing material placement
  3. Reference the relevant Design Guidelines section (C or D)
  4. For stucco: include composition details and application method
  5. For new brick/stone: include photos of similar applications
  6. Submit to Parten Ranch Reviewer and allow 30 days for approval
  7. Do not order or install materials until you receive written approval

Common Misconceptions About Materials

Myth: "I can paint my brick any color I like." Reality: Paint color must be approved by the Parten Ranch Reviewer. Vivid or bright colors are prohibited, and earth tones are required to match the Hill Country aesthetic.

Myth: "Stucco is the same as masonry." Reality: Stucco counts as masonry but is limited to 50% maximum unless approved otherwise. Additionally, stucco composition and application method must be pre-approved.

Myth: "I don't need approval if I'm just repainting." Reality: Paint color changes require approval. You cannot simply repaint in a different color without Parten Ranch Reviewer sign-off.

19. Exterior Lighting

▼

Approval Requirements (Design Guidelines Section K)

All Exterior Lighting Requires Approval: Every exterior light fixture, including accent lighting, must be submitted to and approved by the Parten Ranch Reviewer before installation

Dark Sky Compliance (Design Guidelines Section K)

All exterior lighting must be designed and located to conform to "Dark Sky" principles and the City of Dripping Springs Lighting Ordinance. This means:

No Light Trespass: Lighting must be directed downward and away from adjacent properties. Light must not spill onto neighbors' properties or into the night sky.
Dark Sky Principles: Lighting design must avoid sky glow (light reflecting into the night sky), glare (excessive brightness), light clutter (too many fixtures), and energy waste (unnecessary lighting).

Required Illumination Areas

Proper illumination is required (unless waived by the Reviewer) for common areas, walkways between buildings, parking areas, and entryways/gates.

Prohibited Lighting

No Sports Lighting: Lighting designed to illuminate sports courts, basketball courts, tennis courts, or similar recreational areas is prohibited

Holiday Lighting

Holiday decorative lighting is generally permitted but should be temporary (removed within 30 days after the holiday), should not create excessive light trespass onto adjacent properties, and should use warm-colored lights (amber/warm white preferred).

Fixture Guidance

When submitting lighting plans for approval, consider these design elements:

  • Downward-directed fixtures only (no uplighting or floodlighting)
  • Shielded fixtures to prevent spillage
  • Warm color temperature (2700K or lower) preferred
  • Fixtures that are architecturally consistent with your home's design
  • Minimalist approach (fewer, well-placed fixtures rather than excessive lighting)

City of Dripping Springs Compliance

The City of Dripping Springs has its own lighting ordinance. Your design must comply with both the HOA requirements AND city requirements. In case of conflict, the stricter standard applies.

Application Process for Outdoor Lighting

  1. Identify the specific lighting needs (entry, driveway, accent, etc.)
  2. Select fixtures that are downward-directed and shielded
  3. Collect product specifications and photos of proposed fixtures
  4. Create a site plan showing fixture locations
  5. Include color temperature specifications (recommend 2700K or lower)
  6. Submit to Parten Ranch Reviewer with statement confirming Dark Sky compliance
  7. Obtain approval before purchasing or installing any fixtures

Common Lighting Mistakes

Myth: "I can install motion-sensor lights without approval." Reality: All exterior lighting requires Parten Ranch Reviewer approval, including motion-sensor fixtures. Submit the specific product for approval before installation.

Myth: "Bright white lights are better for security." Reality: Warm-color (2700K) lighting is preferred and provides adequate illumination while maintaining Dark Sky compliance.

20. Home Business & Rental Rules

▼

Home Business - IS Allowed (Declaration Section 2.1)

Home-Based Business Permitted: Owners and occupants MAY conduct business activities within a residence, provided ALL seven conditions below are met

Seven Required Conditions (Declaration Section 2.1)

1. Limited Participation: Only the owner(s) or occupant(s) of the residence may participate. You cannot hire employees to work in your home.
2. Not Apparent or Detectable: The business must NOT be apparent or detectable by sight, sound, or smell from outside the residence. No business signs, no visible work activity, no unusual smells.
3. No Door-to-Door Solicitation: The business cannot involve soliciting other residents in the community.
4. Normal Traffic Levels: The business cannot generate noticeably greater vehicular or pedestrian traffic compared to typical residential properties.
5. Consistent with Residential Character: The business must be consistent with the residential character of the community, not a nuisance, not hazardous or offensive, and not threatening to the security or safety of other residents.
6. No Unusual Machinery: No installation of machinery other than that customary to normal household operations. No manufacturing, heavy equipment, or specialized industrial equipment.
7. Property Not Open to Public: The residence cannot be considered "open to the public" for any business or commercial license. Clients must be by private appointment, not walk-ins.

Rental Rules (Declaration Section 2.2)

Minimum 6-Month Lease: All rentals must be for a term of at least six months. Short-term rentals (Airbnb, VRBO, vacation rentals) are prohibited.
Entire Property Only: You cannot rent out individual rooms or portions of the property. The entire lot and improvements must be rented as a unit.
Written Lease Required: All leases must be in writing (not verbal or informal agreements).
HOA Notification Within 10 Days: You must notify the HOA within 10 days after the lease starts, including tenant info and lease dates.
Tenant Gets HOA Docs: You must provide the tenant with copies of all governing documents (Declaration, Design Guidelines, Community Manual). The owner remains liable for tenant violations.

Common Misconceptions

Myth: "I can Airbnb my house when I'm on vacation." Reality: All rentals must be minimum 6 months. Short-term rentals of any kind are prohibited.

Myth: "I can rent out my guest house separately." Reality: You cannot rent a portion of the lot. The entire property must be rented as a unit to a single tenant household.

Myth: "My tenant's violations are their problem." Reality: The owner is liable for tenant conduct. The HOA can fine you for your tenant's violations and even has the right to evict the tenant.
